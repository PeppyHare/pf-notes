    Magnetohydrodynamic (MHD) Model

    Applying approximations to the two-fluid plasma model will allow us to arrive at a single-fluid (center-of-mass) description. The result is the ideal magnetohydrodynamic model (MHD).

    First, define the MHD variables:

    mass density:ρ=nimi+nime \text{mass density:} \qquad \rho = n_i m_i + n_i m_e mass density:ρ=ni​mi​+ni​me​

    fluid velocity:v=nimivi+nemevenimi+neme \text{fluid velocity:} \qquad \vec v = \frac{n_i m_i \vec v_i + n_e m_e \vec v_e}{n_i m_i + n_e m_e} fluid velocity:v=ni​mi​+ne​me​ni​mi​vi​+ne​me​ve​​

    current density:j=qinivi−eneve→ve=qiniviene−j/ene \text{current density:} \qquad \vec j = q_i n_i \vec v_i - e n_e \vec v_e \rightarrow \vec v_e = \frac{q_i n_i \vec v_i}{e n_e} - \vec j / e n_e current density:j=qi​ni​vi​−ene​ve​→ve​=ene​qi​ni​vi​​−j/ene​

    total pressure:p=pi+pe=neTe+niTi \text{total pressure:} \qquad p = p_i + p_e = n_e T_e + n_i T_i total pressure:p=pi​+pe​=ne​Te​+ni​Ti​

    total temperature:T=niTi+neTe(ni+ne)/2 \text{total temperature:} \qquad T = \frac{n_i T_i + n_e T_e}{(n_i + n_e)/2} total temperature:T=(ni​+ne​)/2ni​Ti​+ne​Te​​

    Now let's begin applying asymptotic approximations. For the mass density, applying the first approx (charge neutrality) we have

    ρ≈n(mi+me) \rho \approx n (m_i + m_e) ρ≈n(mi​+me​)

    Using approx 2 (vanishing electron mass)

    ρ≈nmi \rho \approx n m_i ρ≈nmi​

    where

    n=nine=Zn n = n_i \qquad n_e = Z n n=ni​ne​=Zn

    The center-of-mass velocity (charge neutrality) gives

    v≈mivi+meZvemi+Zme \vec v \approx \frac{m_i \vec v_i + m_e Z \vec v_e}{m_i + Z m_e} v≈mi​+Zme​mi​vi​+me​Zve​​

    with small electron mass approximation:

    v≈vi \vec v \approx \vec v_i v≈vi​

    The current density is (charge neutrality approx)

    j≈Zen(vi−ve)ve=vi−j/Zen \vec j \approx Z e n (\vec v_i - \vec v_e) \qquad \vec v_e = \vec v_i - \vec j / Z e n j≈Zen(vi​−ve​)ve​=vi​−j/Zen

    The pressure and total temperature are (with charge neutrality)

    P≈n(Ti+ZTe) P \approx n (T_i + Z T_e) P≈n(Ti​+ZTe​)

    T≈Ti+ZTe(1+Z)/2 T \approx \frac{T_i + Z T_e}{(1 + Z)/2} T≈(1+Z)/2Ti​+ZTe​​

    Ideal MHD Model

    The extended MHD equations are simpler than the two-fluid model, but they can still be quite complicated. We can often still get useful analysis from further reductions. The ideal MHD model is such a reduction that we can get by dropping (with justification) several terms from the extended model. We justify the simplifications by comparing the magnitude of the neglected terms to the terms that are retained.

    Recall the characteristic speed is vT,i v_{T, i} vT,i​. If we say that the characteristic length plasma length is L L L, then we can define characteristic time τ=L/vT,i \tau = L / v_{T, i} τ=L/vT,i​.

    The derivation of the two-fluid plasma model assumed a Maxwellian velocity distribution. We need the velocity distribution to thermalize, reach local thermodynamic equilibrium, and become Maxwellian. This means that we need many collisions, in fact so many collisions occurring frequently enough that we can ignore collisional effects. There must then be many collisions during the characteristic time τ \tau τ.

    For ions to be thermalized,

    τiiτ≪1 \frac{\tau_{ii}}{\tau} \ll 1 ττii​​≪1

    And similarly for electrons

    τeτ≪1 \frac{\tau_{e}}{\tau} \ll 1 ττe​​≪1

    The continuity equation remains unchanged from the extended MHD model

    Ideal MHD Continuity Equation

    ∂ρ∂t+∇⋅(ρv)=0 \pdv{\rho}{t} + \div (\rho \vec v) = 0 ∂t∂ρ​+∇⋅(ρv)=0

    The momentum equation is

    ρ(∂v∂t+v⋅∇v)+∇p−j×B=−∇⋅(Πe+Πe) \rho \left( \pdv{\vec v}{t} + \vec v \cdot \grad \vec v \right) + \grad p - \vec j \cross \vec B = - \div (\vec \Pi_e + \vec \Pi_e) ρ(∂t∂v​+v⋅∇v)+∇p−j×B=−∇⋅(Πe​+Πe​)

    Drop the anisotropic pressure

    Ideal MHD Momentum Equation

    ρ(∂v∂t+v⋅∇v)+∇p−j×B=0 \rho \left( \pdv{\vec v}{t} + \vec v \cdot \grad \vec v \right) + \grad p - \vec j \cross \vec B = 0 ρ(∂t∂v​+v⋅∇v)+∇p−j×B=0

    The generalized Ohm's law is

    E+v×B=ηj+1Zen(j×B−∇pe−∇⋅Πe) \vec E + \vec v \cross \vec B = \eta \vec j + \frac{1}{Z e n} \left( \vec j \cross B - \grad p_e - \div \vec \Pi_e \right) E+v×B=ηj+Zen1​(j×B−∇pe​−∇⋅Πe​)

    We're going to drop the entire right hand side

    Ideal MHD Generalized Ohm's Law

    If we assume that the ions and electrons are in thermal equilibriumTi=Te T_i = T_e Ti​=Te​, we can relate the collision times

    τee:τii:τei=1:(mime)1/2:mime \tau_{ee} : \tau_{ii} : \tau_{ei} = 1 : \left( \frac{m_i}{m_e} \right) ^{1/2} : \frac{m_i}{m_e} τee​:τii​:τei​=1:(me​mi​​)1/2:me​mi​​

    Perpendicular MHD

    For most configurations for magnetic fusion confinement, we are able to satisfy 2. and 3. but often have densities much too low to meet the high collisionality constraint. However, in practice ideal MHD does accurately model macroscopic behavior of many plasmas. At the same time, magnetized / fusion plasmas are often largely collisionless. We can understand why by re-writing the collisionality requirement as

    1≫τiiτ=τiivT,iτvT,i∼λL 1 \gg \frac{\tau_{ii}}{\tau} = \frac{\tau_{ii} v_{T, i}}{\tau v_{T, i}} \sim \frac{\lambda}{L} 1≫ττii​​=τvT,i​τii​vT,i​​∼Lλ​

    where λ \lambda λ is the mean free path. The ratio λ/L \lambda / L λ/L is also known as the Knudsen number. In magnetized plasmas, the mean free path is often very long, but the path between collisions can cover a great distance only by following magnetic field lines. Motion ⊥ \perp ⊥ to the magnetic field is constrained by rL,i r_{L, i} rL,i​. This suggests an approach wherein we divide the plasma model into a 1-D kinetic model to be solved along the magnetic field and a 2-D MHD model ⊥ \perp ⊥ to the magnetic field.

    We consider diffusivity (terms like viscosity, conductivity) parallel and perpendicular to B \vec B B

    k∥∼λ2τii k_\parallel \sim \frac{\lambda ^2}{\tau_{ii}} k∥​∼τii​λ2​

    k∥k⊥∼(λrL,i)2∼(ωc,iτii)2 \frac{k_\parallel}{k_\perp} \sim \left( \frac{\lambda}{r_{L, i}} \right)^2 \sim (\omega_{c, i} \tau_{ii} )^2 k⊥​k∥​​∼(rL,i​λ​)2∼(ωc,i​τii​)2

    Collisionless MHD reproduces many of the effects of ideal MHD but has a wider region of validity. Corollary: ideal MHD is accurate beyond its region of validity, unless results lead to parallel gradients. For example, we know that MHD is not valid when representing confinement of a plasma confined in a magnetic mirror, which is an inherently kinetic phenomenon. But ideal MHD can generate parallel gradients within its region of validity, and we need to be careful. Ideal MHD does not require different models ∥ \parallel ∥ and ⊥ \perp ⊥ to the magnetic field, and is therefore preferred. We will continue to use ideal MHD outside of its region of validity.
