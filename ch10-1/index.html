    Maxwell-Boltzmann Distribution Function

    Suppose we've got a large number N of particles with total energy W W W. What is the energy distribution among the particles?

    We call a "state" a possible distribution (permutation) of energy among the particles which satisfies the constraints. The fundamental principle of statistical mechanics states that all states have an equal chance of being occupied, with the resulting combinatorial factor giving the distribution. Because the number of particles is very large, the distribution will be close to the distribution that has the largest number of states.

    Label the particles by the energy they have. Let ni n_i ni​ be the number of particles with energy between ϵi \epsilon_i ϵi​ and ϵi+1=ϵi+δϵ \epsilon_{i+1} = \epsilon_i + \delta \epsilon ϵi+1​=ϵi​+δϵ. Choose k k k different energy ranges:

    ∑i=0kni=N∑i=1kϵini=W= total energy \sum_{i = 0} ^k n_i = N \qquad \sum_{i=1} ^k \epsilon_i n_i = W = \text{ total energy} i=0∑k​ni​=Ni=1∑k​ϵi​ni​=W= total energy

    The number of states with a given distribution is just a partitioning (indistinguishable!) of the energy among k k k bins, so

    P=1NN!n1!…nk!=N−1!n1!…nk!≈N!n1!…nk! P = \frac{1}{N} \frac{N!}{n_1 ! \ldots n_k ! } = \frac{N-1!}{n_1 ! \ldots n_k ! } \approx \frac{N!}{n_1 ! \ldots n_k ! } P=N1​n1​!…nk​!N!​=n1​!…nk​!N−1!​≈n1​!…nk​!N!​

    We know that the distribution will be closely centered about the maximum of P, subject to the conservation constraints of energy and particles

    δN=0=∑1kδni \delta N = 0 = \sum _1 ^ k \delta n_i δN=0=1∑k​δni​

    δW=0=∑1kϵiδni \delta W = 0 = \sum_1 ^k \epsilon_i \delta n_i δW=0=1∑k​ϵi​δni​

    Going back to our log trick to find the critical point

    δln⁡P=0 \delta \ln P = 0 δlnP=0

    ln⁡P=ln⁡N!−∑1kln⁡ni! \ln P = \ln N! - \sum_1 ^k \ln n_i ! lnP=lnN!−1∑k​lnni​!

    δln⁡P=−∑1kln⁡niδni=0 \delta \ln P = - \sum_1 ^k \ln n_i \delta n_i = 0 δlnP=−1∑k​lnni​δni​=0

    Let λ \lambda λ be the Lagrange multiplier for δN \delta N δN and β \beta β be the Lagrange multiplier for δW \delta W δW, so

    (ln⁡ni+λ+βϵi)δni=0 (\ln n_i + \lambda + \beta \epsilon_i) \delta n_i = 0 (lnni​+λ+βϵi​)δni​=0

    ln⁡ni=0λ−βϵi \ln n_i = 0 \lambda - \beta \epsilon_i lnni​=0λ−βϵi​

    ∫0∞nλe−βϵdϵ=N \int _0 ^\infty n_\lambda e^{- \beta \epsilon} \dd \epsilon = N ∫0∞​nλ​e−βϵdϵ=N

    ∫0∞ϵnλe−βϵdϵ=W \int_0 ^\infty \epsilon n_\lambda e^{- \beta \epsilon} \dd \epsilon = W ∫0∞​ϵnλ​e−βϵdϵ=W

    Example: Distribution under Gravity

    If we have a bunch of particles under the influence of gravity, the energy is

    ϵ=12mv2+mgz \epsilon = \frac{1}{2} m v^2 + mgz ϵ=21​mv2+mgz

    The distribution is

    f(v)=n0(m2πkT)3/2e−(12mv2+mgz)/kT f(v) = n_0 \left( \frac{m}{2 \pi k T} \right) ^{3/2} e^{-( \frac{1}{2} m v^2 + mgz)/kT} f(v)=n0​(2πkTm​)3/2e−(21​mv2+mgz)/kT

    n(z)=n0e−mgz/kT n(z) = n_0 e^{- mgz / kT} n(z)=n0​e−mgz/kT
