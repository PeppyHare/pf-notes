    Two-Fluid Plasma Model (ions-electrons)

    The two-fluid plasma model is highly reduced from the full kinetic model, but it is still too complete to be useful when studying gross plasma behavior. Further reductions of the model are possible by making asymptotic assumptions:

    Low-frequency Asymptotic Assumption

    • Eliminate high frequency, short wavelength phenomena by using pre-Maxwell field equations. Formally, this is ϵ0→0 \epsilon_0 \rightarrow 0 ϵ0​→0.

    The direct consequences of the low-frequency approximation are

    c2=1ϵ0μ0c→∞ c^2 = \frac{1}{\epsilon_0 \mu_0} \qquad c \rightarrow \infty c2=ϵ0​μ0​1​c→∞

    ωp2=ne2ϵ0mωp→∞ \omega_p ^2 = \frac{n e^2}{\epsilon_0 m} \qquad \omega_p \rightarrow \infty ωp2​=ϵ0​mne2​ωp​→∞

    λD=vTωp→0 \lambda_D = \frac{v_T}{\omega_p} \rightarrow 0 λD​=ωp​vT​​→0

    This means that all phenomena will have ω≪ωp \omega \ll \omega_p ω≪ωp​, limiting the frequencies we can resolve to the ion plasma frequency. The characteristic speeds will be limited by the speed of light

    ωk≪c \frac{\omega}{k} \ll c kω​≪c

    and all characteristic lengths will be much greater than the Debye length

    x0≫λD x_0 \gg \lambda_D x0​≫λD​

    Looking at Gauss' law,

    ϵ0∇⋅E=∑αqαnα→∑αqαnα=0 \epsilon_0 \div \vec E = \sum_\alpha q_\alpha n_\alpha \rightarrow \sum_\alpha q_\alpha n_\alpha = 0 ϵ0​∇⋅E=α∑​qα​nα​→α∑​qα​nα​=0

    so we now have charge neutrality everywhere in the domain. For H plasma, locally we have ne=ni n_e = n_i ne​=ni​ everywhere.

    Looking at Ampere's law,

    ϵ0∂E∂t=1μ0∇×B−∑αqαnαvα=0→j=1μ0∇×B \epsilon_0 \pdv{\vec E}{t} = \frac{1}{\mu_0} \curl \vec B - \sum_\alpha q_\alpha n_\alpha \vec v_\alpha = 0 \\ \rightarrow \vec j = \frac{1}{\mu_0} \curl \vec B ϵ0​∂t∂E​=μ0​1​∇×B−α∑​qα​nα​vα​=0→j=μ0​1​∇×B

    Things we do not get from this approximation are E=0 \vec E = 0 E=0 or ∂E∂t=0 \pdv{\vec E}{t} = 0 ∂t∂E​=0. It does mean that plasma dynamics occur on a sufficiently large spatial scale that charge separation is small, and they occur on a sufficiently long temporal scale that electrons respond quickly.

    Tiny electron asymptotic assumption

    2nd approximation: neglect electron inertia in the momentum equation. Formally, we let the electron mass me→0 m_e \rightarrow 0 me​→0

    ωpe2=ne2ϵ0me→∞ \omega_{pe}^2 = \frac{n e^2}{\epsilon_0 m_e} \rightarrow \infty ωpe2​=ϵ0​me​ne2​→∞

    ωc,e=eBme→∞ \omega_{c, e} = \frac{e B}{m_e} \rightarrow \infty ωc,e​=me​eB​→∞

    vT,e→∞ v_{T, e} \rightarrow \infty vT,e​→∞

    The Larmor radius goes to zero

    rl,e=vT,eωc,e→0 r_{l, e} = \frac{v_{T, e}}{\omega_{c, e}} \rightarrow 0 rl,e​=ωc,e​vT,e​​→0

    Importantly, as the gyroradius rl,e r_{l, e} rl,e​ goes to 0 (because the thermal velocity goes as me \sqrt{m_e} me​​ and the cyclotron frequency goes as me m_e me​), this means that the electrons are tied to the magnetic field. The skin depth is also small.

    δe=cωp,e→0 \delta_e = \frac{c}{\omega_{p, e}} \rightarrow 0 δe​=ωp,e​c​→0

    So all phenomena that we capture must have ω≪ωp,e \omega \ll \omega_{p, e} ω≪ωp,e​, ω≪ωc,e \omega \ll \omega_{c, e} ω≪ωc,e​, and x0≫rL,e x_0 \gg r_{L, e} x0​≫rL,e​, x0≫δe x_0 \gg \delta_e x0​≫δe​ .

    The electron momentum equation becomes

    ∇Pe+∇⋅Πe+ene(E+v×B)=∑β≠αRαβ \grad P_e + \div \vec \Pi _e + e n_e (\vec E + \vec v \cross \vec B) = \sum_{\beta \neq \alpha} \vec R_{\alpha \beta} ∇Pe​+∇⋅Πe​+ene​(E+v×B)=β​=α∑​Rαβ​

    The momentum equation is now a state equation, not an evolution equation. It now simply relates the dynamical variables to each other at any point in time.

    Now, note that along magnetic field lines electrons can travel long distances at very fast (finite) speeds which can produce low frequency, long wavelength phenomena. Neglecting electron inertia implies that electrons respond instantaneously, meaning we cannot capture these modes. An example of such a phenomena is drift waves.

    The characteristic speeds c c c and vT,e v_{T, e} vT,e​ have disappeared from the model. Remaining is vT,i v_{T, i} vT,i​. This means that the ion dynamics dictate the plasma evolution.
