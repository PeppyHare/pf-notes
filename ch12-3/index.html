    Plasma Fluid Model

    We take velocity moments of each of the pieces of the kinetic model: - distribution function, fα f_\alpha fα​ → \rightarrow → fluid variables - Boltzmann Equation → \rightarrow → governing equations describing the evolution of the fluid variables.

    Starting with the zeroth moment (integral) of the distribution function:

    ∫fα(x,v,t)dv=nα(x,t) \int f_\alpha (\vec x, \vec v, t) \dd \vec v = n_\alpha(\vec x, t) ∫fα​(x,v,t)dv=nα​(x,t)

    1st Moment (momentum):

    mα∫v⋅fα(x,v,t)dv=pα(x,t)=mαnαvα m_\alpha \int \vec v \cdot f_\alpha(\vec x, \vec v, t) \dd \vec v = \vec p_\alpha (\vec x, t) = m_\alpha n_\alpha \vec v_\alpha mα​∫v⋅fα​(x,v,t)dv=pα​(x,t)=mα​nα​vα​

    which is to say that the velocity is the 1st moment divided by the zeroth moment

    vα=∫vfα∫fα v_\alpha = \frac{\int \vec v f_\alpha}{\int f_\alpha} vα​=∫fα​∫vfα​​

    2nd Moment:

    ∫vvfα(x,v,t)dv=Eα(x,t)(energy tensor) \int \vec v \vec v f_\alpha (\vec x, \vec v, t) \dd \vec v = \vec E_\alpha(\vec x, t) (\text{energy tensor}) ∫vvfα​(x,v,t)dv=Eα​(x,t)(energy tensor)

    We can simplify the 2nd moment by taking a reduced 2nd moment. This means that we're going to insert a dot product

    ∫v⋅vfα(x,v,t)dv \int \vec v \cdot \vec v f_\alpha (\vec x, \vec v, t) \dd \vec v ∫v⋅vfα​(x,v,t)dv

    Before moving forward, we want to define a "random" velocity. Note that

    ∫vfα−vα∫fα=0→∫(v−vα)fαdv=0 \int \vec v f_\alpha - \vec v_\alpha \int f_\alpha = 0 \rightarrow \int (\vec v - \vec v_\alpha) f_\alpha \dd \vec v = 0 ∫vfα​−vα​∫fα​=0→∫(v−vα​)fα​dv=0

    We can define a random velocity w=v−vα \vec w = \vec v - \vec v_\alpha w=v−vα​. It is random in the sense that it is a fluctuation about the mean velocity, and when we integrate it we get zero. We can use this to define the energy tensor using the mean velocity to get a meaningful result. The pressure tensor is the second moment, using the random velocity

    Pα=mα∫wwfαdv=PαI‾+Π‾α \vec P_\alpha = m_\alpha \int \vec w \vec w f_\alpha \dd \vec v = P_\alpha \overline{I} + \overline{\Pi}_\alpha Pα​=mα​∫wwfα​dv=Pα​I+Πα​

    where we've decomposed the pressure into an isotropic value Pα P_\alpha Pα​ and what's called the Braginskii stress tensor Π‾α \overline{\Pi}_\alpha Πα​. The average isotropic pressure is given by the reduced 2nd moment:

    Pα=nαTα=13∫mαw⋅wfα P_\alpha = n_\alpha T_\alpha = \frac{1}{3} \int m_\alpha \vec w \cdot \vec w f_\alpha Pα​=nα​Tα​=31​∫mα​w⋅wfα​

    where the factor of 1/3 1/3 1/3 comes from the number of degrees of freedom in our system. It is related to the thermodynamic factor γ \gamma γ where γ=DOF+2DOF \gamma = \frac{DOF + 2}{DOF} γ=DOFDOF+2​. Now we can define the temperature Tα T_\alpha Tα​ as

    Tα(x,t)=1DOF∫mαw⋅wfαdv∫fαdv T_\alpha (\vec x, t) = \frac{1}{DOF} \frac{\int m_\alpha \vec w \cdot \vec w f_\alpha \dd \vec v}{\int f_\alpha \dd \vec v} Tα​(x,t)=DOF1​∫fα​dv∫mα​w⋅wfα​dv​

    Now that we've got nα n_\alpha nα​, vα v_\alpha vα​, and Tα T_\alpha Tα​ we have what we need to define a Maxwellian distribution. Higher moments would be required to describe non-Maxwellian distribution functions. For example, the 3rd moment is called the skewness of the distribution. The 4th moment is the kurtosis. So on and so forth. These give a measure of degree of departure from a Maxwellian distribution, in which case it is often more useful to talk about the excess kurtosis, where the excess kurtosis of a Maxwellian is defined to be zero. You can continue to calculate the moment expansion, and in general it requires an infinite number of moments to describe an arbitrary distribution function. Because of the Boltzmann H-theorem and the tendency of plasmas to quickly relax to Maxwellian, we can usually get away with using just the first three moments.

    Now, what are the governing equations? We get these by taking moments of the Boltzmann equation. Let's proceed carefully in sections, so we'll integrate each piece of the BE in terms.

    0th Moment of Boltzmann Equation (Conservation)

    ∫∂fα∂tdv+∫v⋅∂fα∂xdv+∫qαmα(E+v×B)⋅∂fα∂vdv=∫∂fα∂t∣colldv \int \pdv{f_\alpha}{t} \dd \vec v + \int \vec v \cdot \pdv{f_\alpha}{x} \dd v + \int \frac{q_\alpha}{m_\alpha} (\vec E + \vec v \cross \vec B) \cdot \pdv{f_\alpha}{\vec v} \dd \vec v = \int \left. \pdv{f_\alpha}{t} \right|_{coll} \dd \vec v ∫∂t∂fα​​dv+∫v⋅∂x∂fα​​dv+∫mα​qα​​(E+v×B)⋅∂v∂fα​​dv=∫∂t∂fα​​∣∣∣∣∣​coll​dv

    First, we take ∫∂fα∂tdv \int \pdv{f_\alpha}{t} \dd \vec v ∫∂t∂fα​​dv. Because t and v are both independent variables, with an argument of sufficient smoothness we can reverse the order of integration and differentiation

    ∫∂fα∂tdv=∂∂t∫fαdv=∂nα∂t \int \pdv{f_\alpha}{t} \dd \vec v = \pdv{}{t}\int f_\alpha \dd v = \pdv{n_\alpha}{t} ∫∂t∂fα​​dv=∂t∂​∫fα​dv=∂t∂nα​​

    For ∫v⋅∂fα∂xdv \int \vec v \cdot \pdv{f_\alpha}{x} \dd v ∫v⋅∂x∂fα​​dv we can perform an integration by parts

    ∫v⋅∂fα∂xdv=∫∂∂x⋅(vfα)dv−∫fα∂∂x⋅vdv=∫∂∂x⋅(vfα)dv=∂∂x⋅∫vfαdv=∂∂x⋅(nαvα)=∇⋅(nαvα) \int \vec v \cdot \pdv{f_\alpha}{x} \dd v = \int \pdv{}{\vec x} \cdot ( \vec v f_\alpha) \dd \vec v - \int f_\alpha \pdv{}{\vec x} \cdot \vec v \dd \vec v \\ = \int \pdv{}{\vec x} \cdot ( \vec v f_\alpha) \dd \vec v \\ = \pdv{}{\vec x} \cdot \int \vec v f_\alpha \dd \vec v \\ = \pdv{}{\vec x} \cdot (n_\alpha \vec v_\alpha) = \div (n_\alpha \vec v_\alpha) ∫v⋅∂x∂fα​​dv=∫∂x∂​⋅(vfα​)dv−∫fα​∂x∂​⋅vdv=∫∂x∂​⋅(vfα​)dv=∂x∂​⋅∫vfα​dv=∂x∂​⋅(nα​vα​)=∇⋅(nα​vα​)

    Once again, we've switched the order of integration of x x x and v v v, which we can only do because we have specified that f f f is a distribution function, and as such meets the criterion of sufficient smoothness.

    For the last part, we can write it as a surface integral

    ∫qαmα(E+v×B)⋅∂fα∂vdv=∮fαqαmα(E+v×B)⋅dSv−∫qαmαfα∂∂v⋅(E+v×B)dv \int \frac{q_\alpha}{m_\alpha} (\vec E + \vec v \cross \vec B) \cdot \pdv{f_\alpha}{\vec v} \dd \vec v = \oint f_\alpha \frac{q_\alpha}{m_\alpha} ( \vec E + \vec v \cross \vec B) \cdot \dd S_v - \int \frac{q_\alpha}{m_\alpha} f_\alpha \pdv{}{\vec v} \cdot (\vec E + \vec v \cross \vec B) \dd \vec v ∫mα​qα​​(E+v×B)⋅∂v∂fα​​dv=∮fα​mα​qα​​(E+v×B)⋅dSv​−∫mα​qα​​fα​∂v∂​⋅(E+v×B)dv

    For fα f_\alpha fα​ to be well-defined, we require lim⁡v→∞v3fα=0 \lim_{v \rightarrow \infty} v^3 f_\alpha = 0 limv→∞​v3fα​=0 so the surface term vanishes and we're left with

    ∫qαmα(E+v×B)⋅∂fα∂vdv=−∫qαmαfα∂∂v⋅(E+v×B)dv \int \frac{q_\alpha}{m_\alpha} (\vec E + \vec v \cross \vec B) \cdot \pdv{f_\alpha}{\vec v} \dd \vec v = - \int \frac{q_\alpha}{m_\alpha} f_\alpha \pdv{}{\vec v} \cdot (\vec E + \vec v \cross \vec B) \dd \vec v ∫mα​qα​​(E+v×B)⋅∂v∂fα​​dv=−∫mα​qα​​fα​∂v∂​⋅(E+v×B)dv

    Distributing the divergence through,

    ∂∂v⋅E=0 \pdv{}{\vec v} \cdot \vec E = 0 ∂v∂​⋅E=0

    because E \vec E E is independent of v \vec v v.

    ∂∂v⋅(v×B)=0 \pdv{}{\vec v} \cdot ( \vec v \cross \vec B) = 0 ∂v∂​⋅(v×B)=0

    because v×B \vec v \cross \vec B v×B is always orthogonal to v \vec v v.

    We've been working through this in normal vector notation for familiarity, but there is another notation known as Einstein Tensor Notation using the lovely Levi-Civita symbol ϵijk \epsilon_{ijk} ϵijk​.

    ϵijk=1even permutations of i j k=−1odd permutations of i j k=0any repeated indexes \epsilon_{ijk} = 1 \qquad \text{even permutations of i j k} \\ = -1 \qquad \text{odd permutations of i j k} \\ = 0 \qquad \text{any repeated indexes} ϵijk​=1even permutations of i j k=−1odd permutations of i j k=0any repeated indexes

    We can write out vector products as products of indices and operators, and any repeated indices are implicitly summed:

    v×B=ϵijkvjBk=∑jkϵijkvjBk \vec v \cross \vec B = \epsilon_{ijk} v_j B_k = \sum_{jk} \epsilon_{ijk} v_j B_k v×B=ϵijk​vj​Bk​=jk∑​ϵijk​vj​Bk​

    where ϵijk \epsilon_{ijk} ϵijk​ is defined to be 1 for even permutations of ijk, -1 for negative permutations if ijk, and 0 for any repeated indices.

    We can now write the derivative with respect to v \vec v v as

    ∂∂v⋅v×B=∂viϵijkvjBk=ϵijk(∂vivj)Bk \pdv{}{\vec v} \cdot \vec v \cross \vec B = \partial _{v_i} \epsilon_{ijk} v_j B_k \\ = \epsilon_{ijk} (\partial_{v_i} v_j) B_k ∂v∂​⋅v×B=∂vi​​ϵijk​vj​Bk​=ϵijk​(∂vi​​vj​)Bk​

    We see that we're taking the derivative of the j-th component of velocity with respect to the i-th component of velocity, and that's how we can most easily point out that the quantity is zero without relying on properties of 3-vector products.

    We'll also want to use the divergence in Einstein tensor notation

    ∇⋅A=∂iAjδij=∂iAi \div \vec A = \partial_i A_j \delta_{ij} = \partial_i A_i ∇⋅A=∂i​Aj​δij​=∂i​Ai​

    Finally, we come back to the collision term in the zeroth moment of the B-M equation

    ∑β∫Cαβdv=0 \sum_\beta \int C_{\alpha \beta} \dd \vec v = 0 β∑​∫Cαβ​dv=0

    We can say the collision term is zero by making a physical argument, rather than a mathematical one. We assert that collisions cannot create or destroy particles. This assumption is now baked into our equations going forward, but is not always true! Ionization, recombination, fusion reactions all create/destroy species.

    Finally, we've got

    ∂nα∂t+∇⋅(nαvα)=0continuity equation \pdv{n_\alpha}{t} + \div ( n_\alpha \vec v_\alpha) = 0 \qquad \text{continuity equation} ∂t∂nα​​+∇⋅(nα​vα​)=0continuity equation

    So by taking the 0th moment of the Boltzmann Equation, we've arrived at the continuity equation for nα n_\alpha nα​ by introducing vα \vec v_\alpha vα​.

    1st Moment of Boltzmann Equation

    ∫v∂fα∂tdv+∫vv∂fα∂xdv+∫vqαmα(E+v×B)⋅∂fα∂vdv=∫v∂fα∂t∣coll \int \vec v \pdv{f_\alpha}{t} \dd \vec v + \int \vec v \vec v \pdv{f_\alpha}{\vec x} \dd \vec v + \int \vec v \frac{q_\alpha}{m_\alpha} (\vec E + \vec v \cross \vec B) \cdot \pdv{f_\alpha}{\vec v} \dd \vec v = \int \left. \vec v \pdv{f_\alpha}{t} \right|_{coll} ∫v∂t∂fα​​dv+∫vv∂x∂fα​​dv+∫vmα​qα​​(E+v×B)⋅∂v∂fα​​dv=∫v∂t∂fα​​∣∣∣∣∣​coll​

    Term 1:

    ∫v∂fα∂tdv=∂∂t∫vfαdv−∫∂v∂tfαdv=∂∂t(nαvα) \int \vec v \pdv{f_\alpha}{t} \dd \vec v = \pdv{}{t} \int \vec v f_\alpha \dd \vec v - \int \pdv{\vec v}{t} f_\alpha \dd \vec v \\ = \pdv{}{t} \left( n_\alpha \vec v_\alpha \right) ∫v∂t∂fα​​dv=∂t∂​∫vfα​dv−∫∂t∂v​fα​dv=∂t∂​(nα​vα​)

    Before we get to the second term, let's have an aside about dyad math. An outer product AB \vec A \vec B AB gives a second-rank tensor. In Cartesian coordinates, it looks like

    AB=[AxBxAxByAxBzAyBxAyByAyBzAzBxAzByAzBz] \vec A \vec B = \begin{bmatrix} A_x B_x & A_x B_y & A_x B_z \\ A_y B_x & A_y B_y & A_y B_z \\ A_z B_x & A_z B_y & A_z B_z \\ \end{bmatrix} AB=⎣⎢⎡​Ax​Bx​Ay​Bx​Az​Bx​​Ax​By​Ay​By​Az​By​​Ax​Bz​Ay​Bz​Az​Bz​​⎦⎥⎤​

    A useful property to do with dot products:

    AB⋅C=A(B⋅C)=(AB)⋅C \vec A \vec B \cdot \vec C = \vec A ( \vec B \cdot \vec C ) = (\vec A \vec B) \cdot \vec C AB⋅C=A(B⋅C)=(AB)⋅C

    (V⋅∇)B=V⋅(∇B) ( \vec V \cdot \grad) \vec B = \vec V \cdot ( \grad \vec B) (V⋅∇)B=V⋅(∇B)

    Back to term 2:

    ∫vv⋅∂fα∂xdv=∫v⋅∂∂x(vfα)dv−∫vfα∂∂x⋅vdv=∫∂∂x⋅(vvfα)dv−∫vfα⋅∂v∂xdv=∂∂x⋅∫vvfαdv \int \vec v \vec v \cdot \pdv{f_\alpha}{\vec x} \dd \vec v = \int \vec v \cdot \pdv{}{\vec x} ( \vec v f_\alpha) \dd \vec v - \int \vec v f_\alpha \pdv{}{\vec x} \cdot \vec v \dd \vec v \\ = \int \pdv{}{\vec x} \cdot ( \vec v \vec v f_\alpha) \dd \vec v - \int \vec v f_\alpha \cdot \pdv{\vec v}{\vec x} \dd \vec v \\ = \pdv{}{\vec x} \cdot \int \vec v \vec v f_\alpha \dd \vec v ∫vv⋅∂x∂fα​​dv=∫v⋅∂x∂​(vfα​)dv−∫vfα​∂x∂​⋅vdv=∫∂x∂​⋅(vvfα​)dv−∫vfα​⋅∂x∂v​dv=∂x∂​⋅∫vvfα​dv

    Re-expanding in terms of random velocity v=vα+w \vec v = \vec v_\alpha + \vec w v=vα​+w

    =∂∂x⋅∫(vα+w)(vα+w)fαdv=∂∂x⋅∫(vαvα+vαw+wvα+ww)fαdv=∂∂x⋅[vαvα∫fαdv+vα∫wfαdv+(∫wfαdv)vα+∫wwfαfαdv] = \pdv{}{\vec x} \cdot \int (\vec v_\alpha + \vec w) (\vec v_\alpha + \vec w) f_\alpha \dd \vec v \\ = \pdv{}{\vec x} \cdot \int (\vec v_\alpha \vec v_\alpha + \vec v_\alpha \vec w + \vec w \vec v_\alpha + \vec w \vec w) f_\alpha \dd \vec v \\ = \pdv{}{\vec x} \cdot \left[ \vec v_\alpha \vec v_\alpha \int f_\alpha \dd \vec v + \vec v_\alpha \int \vec w f_\alpha \dd \vec v + \left( \int \vec w f_\alpha \dd \vec v \right) \vec v_\alpha + \int \vec w \vec w f_\alpha f_\alpha \dd \vec v \right] =∂x∂​⋅∫(vα​+w)(vα​+w)fα​dv=∂x∂​⋅∫(vα​vα​+vα​w+wvα​+ww)fα​dv=∂x∂​⋅[vα​vα​∫fα​dv+vα​∫wfα​dv+(∫wfα​dv)vα​+∫wwfα​fα​dv]

    The middle two terms are zero by the very definition of vα \vec v_\alpha vα​. The random velocity w \vec w w is defined such that the integral of the random velocity over all phase space is zero.

    =∂∂x⋅(vαvαnα)+∂∂x⋅Pαmα=∇⋅(nαvαvα)+1mα∇⋅Pα = \pdv{}{\vec x} \cdot \left( \vec v_\alpha \vec v_\alpha n_\alpha \right) + \pdv{}{\vec x} \cdot \frac{ \vec P_\alpha}{m_\alpha} \\ = \div (n_\alpha \vec v_\alpha \vec v_\alpha) + \frac{1}{m_\alpha} \div \vec P_\alpha =∂x∂​⋅(vα​vα​nα​)+∂x∂​⋅mα​Pα​​=∇⋅(nα​vα​vα​)+mα​1​∇⋅Pα​

    Substituting in the pressure tensor,

    =∇⋅(nαvαvα)+1mα(∇Pα+∇⋅Πα) = \div ( n_\alpha \vec v_\alpha \vec v_\alpha ) + \frac{1}{m_\alpha} \left( \grad P_\alpha + \div \vec \Pi _\alpha \right) =∇⋅(nα​vα​vα​)+mα​1​(∇Pα​+∇⋅Πα​)

    Moving on to term 3:

    ∫vqαmα(E+v×B)⋅∂fα∂vdv=∫qαmα∂∂v⋅[(E+v×B)fα]vdv−∫qαmαfα∂∂v⋅[E+v×B]vdv=∫qαmα∂∂v⋅[fα(E+v×B)v]dv−∫qαmαf(E+vα×B)⋅∂v∂vdv=qαmα∮fα(E+vα×B)v⋅dSv−qαmα[E∫fdvα−B×∫vfαdv]=−qαmαnα(E+vα×B) \int \vec v \frac{q_\alpha}{m_\alpha} (\vec E + \vec v \cross \vec B) \cdot \pdv{f_\alpha}{\vec v} \dd \vec v = \int \frac{q_\alpha}{m_\alpha} \pdv{}{\vec v} \cdot \left[( \vec E + \vec v \cross \vec B) f_\alpha \right] \vec v \dd \vec v - \int \frac{q_\alpha}{m_\alpha} f_\alpha \pdv{}{\vec v} \cdot \left[ \vec E + \vec v \cross \vec B \right] \vec v \dd \vec v \\ = \int \frac{q_\alpha}{m_\alpha} \pdv{}{\vec v} \cdot \left[ f_\alpha (\vec E + \vec v \cross \vec B) \vec v \right] \dd \vec v - \int \frac{q\alpha}{m\alpha} f ( \vec E + \vec v\alpha \cross \vec B) \cdot \pdv{\vec v}{\vec v} \dd \vec v \\ = \frac{q\alpha}{m\alpha} \oint f\alpha ( \vec E + \vec v\alpha \cross \vec B) \vec v \cdot \dd \vec S_v - \frac{q\alpha}{m\alpha} \left[ \vec E \int f \dd \vec v\alpha - \vec B \cross \int \vec v f\alpha \dd \vec v \right] \\ = - \frac{q\alpha}{m\alpha} n\alpha (\vec E + v\alpha \cross \vec B) ∫vmα​qα​​(E+v×B)⋅∂v∂fα​​dv=∫mα​qα​​∂v∂​⋅[(E+v×B)fα​]vdv−∫mα​qα​​fα​∂v∂​⋅[E+v×B]vdv=∫mα​qα​​∂v∂​⋅[fα​(E+v×B)v]dv−∫mαqα​f(E+vα×B)⋅∂v∂v​dv=mαqα​∮fα(E+vα×B)v⋅dSv​−mαqα​[E∫fdvα−B×∫vfαdv]=−mαqα​nα(E+vα×B)

    Finally, the 4th term gives

    Now the continuity, momentum, and energy equations describe the evolution of each fluid α \alpha α. The only velocity information we have retained in taking moments is the centroid vα \vec v_\alpha vα​ and the width Tα T_\alpha Tα​. There are fluid models that do evolve those moments, but we won't touch on those here.

    Closure Problem

    We still need to "close" the fluid model (i.e. solving the Closure Problem). As we have been finding out, each additional moment of the Boltzmann Equation introduces the next-higher moment. In calculating the 0th moment, we introduce the 1st moment, etc. We need to address the closure problem by relating higher moment variables to lower moment variables. Applying a closure scheme (relating variables that are not evolved directly) is usually equivalent to making a statement on heat flow.

    In our model, we are not evolving the heat flux hα \vec h_\alpha hα​, so we need to relate it to one of the other variables that we do evolve directly. We usually do that by writing down a conductivity relation

    hα=−κ∇Tα \vec h_\alpha = - \kappa \grad T_\alpha hα​=−κ∇Tα​

    for some conductivity κ \kappa κ.

    We're also only evolving the scalar pressure, so we need to relate the stress tensor Πα \vec \Pi_\alpha Πα​ to the other variables. We can do that with a viscosity relation

    Πα=ν∇vα \vec \Pi_\alpha = \nu \grad \vec v_\alpha Πα​=ν∇vα​

    for some viscosity ν \nu ν. We call the introduced quantities κ \kappa κ and ν \nu ν the transport coefficients. They are usually derived by performing an expansion of the equations we've already discussed. They are what lead to "nondimensional" numbers that characterize the flow in our system.

    We can also achieve closure by constraining portions of the system. In an isothermal system

    isothermal: T=const.,p∝n \text{isothermal: } \quad T = \text{const.}, p \propto n isothermal: T=const.,p∝n

    adiabatic: p∝nγ \text{adiabatic: } \quad p \propto n^\gamma adiabatic: p∝nγ

    force-free, cold: p=0 \text{force-free, cold: } \quad p = 0 force-free, cold: p=0

    The expressions that lead us to a closure scheme are the equations of state. They are not time-dependent, they simply relate fluid parameters to other properties of the system.

    Maxwell's equations to couple the electromagnetic terms to the fluid variables.

    ∂B∂t=−∇×E \pdv{\vec B}{t} = - \curl \vec E ∂t∂B​=−∇×E

    ϵ0∂E∂t=1μ0∇×B−∑alphaqαnαvα \epsilon_0 \pdv{\vec E}{t} = \frac{1}{\mu_0} \curl \vec B - \sum_{alpha} q_\alpha n_\alpha \vec v_\alpha ϵ0​∂t∂E​=μ0​1​∇×B−alpha∑​qα​nα​vα​

    ϵ0∇⋅E=∑αqαnα \epsilon_0 \div \vec E = \sum_\alpha q_\alpha n_\alpha ϵ0​∇⋅E=α∑​qα​nα​

    ∇⋅B=0 \div \vec B = 0 ∇⋅B=0

    The fluid variables provide the source terms for Ampere's law and Gauss' law, and we've already seen how the electromagnetic fields appear in the momentum equations as source terms for the fluid forces.

    Stopping after the 2nd moment (5N-Moment Plasma Fluid Model)

    What assumptions have we made by stopping here? It's important to know what they are and to recognize what they mean for the fluid model.

    • Each species α \alpha α is well-represented by a Maxwellian with a small perturbation. The pressure tensor is not strictly diagonal and the heat flux is not zero, so the small perturbations are what lead to the transport coefficients. This is due to the process by which we obtain the transport coefficients, called the Chapman-Enskog expansion.
    • Kinetic effects (stream instabilities, counter-flow instabilities, etc.) are not captured.

    The variables of the 5-moment model that we do evolve are

    n,vx,vy,vz,T n, v_x, v_y, v_z, T n,vx​,vy​,vz​,T

    We can take additional moments. The 10N-moment equation evolves, in addition to the quantities in the 5N-moment model, we evolve all of the independent terms of the pressure tensor

    n,vx,vy,vz,Pxx,Pxy,Pxz,Pyy,Pyz,Pzz n, v_x, v_y, v_z, P_{xx}, P_{xy}, P_{xz} , P_{yy} , P_{yz} , P_{zz} n,vx​,vy​,vz​,Pxx​,Pxy​,Pxz​,Pyy​,Pyz​,Pzz​

    The 13-N moment model has everything from the 10N-moment model plus the heat flux tensor hx,hy,hz h_x, h_y, h_z hx​,hy​,hz​
