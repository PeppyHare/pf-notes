    9.2 - Electromagnetic Waves in Vacuum

    9.2.1 Wave Equation for E and B

    Recall Maxwell's equations

    (i)∇⋅D=ρf(Gauss’s law)(ii)∇⋅B=0(Ng’s Law)(iii)∇×E=−∂B∂t(Faraday’s Law)(iv)∇×H=Jf+∂D∂t(Ampere’s Law) \begin{aligned} (\text{i}) & \quad \div \vec{D} = \rho_f \quad \text{(Gauss's law)} \\ (\text{ii}) & \quad \div \vec{B} = 0 \quad \text{(Ng's Law)} \\ (\text{iii}) & \quad \curl \vec{E} = - \pdv{\vec{B}}{t} \quad \text{(Faraday's Law}) \\ (\text{iv}) & \quad \curl \vec{H} = \vec{J}_f + \pdv{\vec{D}}{t} \quad \text{(Ampere's Law)} \end{aligned} (i)(ii)(iii)(iv)​∇⋅D=ρf​(Gauss’s law)∇⋅B=0(Ng’s Law)∇×E=−∂t∂B​(Faraday’s Law)∇×H=Jf​+∂t∂D​(Ampere’s Law)​

    To get to a wave equation from these, we start with a curl of curl and use a standard vector identity

    ∇×(∇×E)=∇(∇⋅E)−∇2E \curl (\curl \vec E) = \grad ( \div \vec E) - \grad ^2 \vec E ∇×(∇×E)=∇(∇⋅E)−∇2E

    Use Faraday's law on the left hand side (and move the spatial derivative through the temporal one), and on the right hand side use Gauss' law to re-write the divergence

    →−∂∂t(∇×B)=1ϵ0∇ρ−∇2E \rightarrow - \pdv{}{t} ( \curl \vec B) = \frac{1}{\epsilon_0} \grad \rho - \grad ^2 \vec E →−∂t∂​(∇×B)=ϵ0​1​∇ρ−∇2E

    →−∂∂t(μ0J+μ0ϵ0∂E∂t)=−μ0∂J∂t−μ0ϵ0∂2E∂t2=∇ρϵ0−∇2E \rightarrow - \pdv{}{t} \left( \mu_0 \vec J + \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \pdv{\vec{E}}{t} \right) = - \mu_0 \pdv{\vec J}{t} - \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial ^2 \vec E}{\partial t ^2} = \frac{\grad \rho}{\epsilon_0} - \grad ^2 \vec E →−∂t∂​(μ0​J+μ0​ϵ0​∂t∂E​)=−μ0​∂t∂J​−μ0​ϵ0​∂t2∂2E​=ϵ0​∇ρ​−∇2E

    →∇2E−μ0ϵ0∂2E∂t2=1ϵ0∇ρ+μ0∂J∂t"non-homogeneous" wave equation for E \rightarrow \grad ^2 \vec E - \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial ^2 \vec E}{\partial t^2} = \frac{1}{\epsilon_0} \grad \rho + \mu_0 \pdv{\vec{J}}{t} \quad \text{"non-homogeneous" wave equation for E} →∇2E−μ0​ϵ0​∂t2∂2E​=ϵ0​1​∇ρ+μ0​∂t∂J​"non-homogeneous" wave equation for E

    In a vacuum, ρ=0 \rho = 0 ρ=0 and J=0 \vec J = 0 J=0 so

    ∇2E−μ0ϵ0∂2E∂t2=0 \grad ^2 \vec E - \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial ^2 \vec E}{\partial t^2} = 0 ∇2E−μ0​ϵ0​∂t2∂2E​=0

    which is just a standard 3D wave equation. We can identify the speed of propagation based on the constant of proportionality v=1μ0ϵ0=c v = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mu_0 \epsilon_0}} = c v=μ0​ϵ0​​1​=c

    We could also have started with ∇×(∇×B) \curl (\curl \vec B) ∇×(∇×B) to obtain

    ∇2B−μ0ϵ0∂2B∂t2=−μ0(∇×J)"non-homogeneous" wave equation for B \grad ^2 \vec B - \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial ^2 \vec B}{\partial t^2} = - \mu_0 (\curl \vec J) \quad \text{"non-homogeneous" wave equation for B} ∇2B−μ0​ϵ0​∂t2∂2B​=−μ0​(∇×J)"non-homogeneous" wave equation for B

    So, in vacuum you get the exact same wave equation

    ∇2B−μ0ϵ0∂2B∂t2=0 \grad ^2 \vec B - \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial ^2 \vec B}{\partial t^2} = 0 ∇2B−μ0​ϵ0​∂t2∂2B​=0

    So, both E \vec E E and B \vec B B must satisfy these wave equations. We know that the wave equations admit certain sets of solutions, but that's not the entire story. We'll see additional constraints on solutions to E \vec E E and B \vec B B due to the fact that the waves need to satisfy all of the Maxwell equations, so E \vec E E and B \vec B B are very intimately linked.

    9.2.2: Monochromatic Plane Waves

    Consider monochromatic sine waves (plane waves) in a single direction, so that all variation happens in the z-direction. Again, using the superposition principle we'll be able to build up more complicated solutions.

    E=E0cos⁡(kz−ωt+δ)B=B0cos⁡(kz−ωt+δ) \vec E = \vec E_0 \cos (k z - \omega t + \delta) \qquad \vec B = \vec B_0 \cos (k z - \omega t + \delta) E=E0​cos(kz−ωt+δ)B=B0​cos(kz−ωt+δ)

    E0=E0,xx^+E0,yy^+E0,zz^ \vec E_0 = E_{0, x} \vu x + E_{0, y} \vu y + E_{0, z} \vu z E0​=E0,x​x^+E0,y​y^​+E0,z​z^

    Let's apply Gauss' law (in vacuum)

    ∇⋅E=0 \div \vec E = 0 ∇⋅E=0

    ∇⋅(E0cos⁡(kz−ωt+δ))=0 \div (\vec E_0 \cos (k z - \omega t + \delta) ) = 0 ∇⋅(E0​cos(kz−ωt+δ))=0

    →∂E0,x∂xcos⁡(kz−ωt+δ)+∂E0,y∂ycos⁡(kz−ωt+δ) \rightarrow \pdv{E_{0, x}}{x} \cos (k z - \omega t + \delta ) + \pdv{E_{0, y}}{y} \cos (kz - \omega t + \delta) →∂x∂E0,x​​cos(kz−ωt+δ)+∂y∂E0,y​​cos(kz−ωt+δ)

    +∂E0,z∂zcos⁡(kz−ωt+δ)+E0,z(−ksin⁡(kt−ωt+δ))=0 + \pdv{E_{0, z}}{z} \cos (kz - \omega t + \delta) + E_{0,z}(- k \sin(kt - \omega t + \delta)) = 0 +∂z∂E0,z​​cos(kz−ωt+δ)+E0,z​(−ksin(kt−ωt+δ))=0

    We've decided that there is no variation in the x- and y-directions, so only the final term survives, and must be equal to zero

    E0,z(−ksin⁡(kt−ωt+δ))=0→E0,z=0 E_{0, z} (- k \sin(kt - \omega t + \delta)) = 0 \quad \rightarrow \quad E_{0,z} = 0 E0,z​(−ksin(kt−ωt+δ))=0→E0,z​=0

    Which is to say that EM plane waves are "transverse" waves.

    =x^(E0,yksin⁡(kz−ωt))−y^(E0,xksin⁡(kz−ωt)) = \vu x ( E_{0, y} k \sin(kz - \omega t)) - \vu y (E_{0, x} k \sin(k z - \omega t)) =x^(E0,y​ksin(kz−ωt))−y^​(E0,x​ksin(kz−ωt))

    −∂B∂t=−x^∂∂tB0,xcos⁡(kz−ωt)−y^∂∂tB0,ycos⁡(kz−ωt) - \pdv{B}{t} = - \vu x \pdv{}{t} B_{0, x} \cos (kz - \omega t) - \vu y \pdv{}{t}B_{0, y} \cos (kz - \omega t) −∂t∂B​=−x^∂t∂​B0,x​cos(kz−ωt)−y^​∂t∂​B0,y​cos(kz−ωt)

    Matching up components and canceling the sine functions,

    →kE0,y=−ωB0,xkE0,x=ωB0,y \rightarrow k E_{0, y} = - \omega B_{0, x} \qquad k E_{0, x} = \omega B_{0, y} →kE0,y​=−ωB0,x​kE0,x​=ωB0,y​

    So every time you have an E \vec E E field, you will have a B \vec B B field in an orthogonal direction - they are mutually orthogonal - and they are in phase, since the proportionality factors are real.

    9.2.3: Energy and Momentum in Electromagnetic Waves

    To repeat, for monochromatic plane waves propagating in the z-direction,

    E=E0cos⁡(kz−ωt+δ)=E0cos⁡(kz−ωt+δ)x^ \vec E = \vec{E_0} \cos ( k z - \omega t + \delta) = E_0 \cos (k z - \omega t + \delta) \vu x E=E0​cos(kz−ωt+δ)=E0​cos(kz−ωt+δ)x^

    B=B0cos⁡(kz−ωt+δ)y^ \vec B = B_0 \cos (kz - \omega t + \delta) \vu y B=B0​cos(kz−ωt+δ)y^​

    and

    E0/B0=c E_0 / B_0 = c E0​/B0​=c

    u=12ϵ0E2+12μ0B2=ϵ0E2=ϵ0E0cos⁡2(kz−ωt+δ) u = \frac{1}{2} \epsilon_0 E^2 + \frac{1}{2\mu_0} B^2 = \epsilon_0 E^2 = \epsilon_0 E_0 \cos ^2 (kz - \omega t + \delta) u=21​ϵ0​E2+2μ0​1​B2=ϵ0​E2=ϵ0​E0​cos2(kz−ωt+δ)

    ⟨u⟩=12ϵ0E02 \langle u \rangle = \frac{1}{2} \epsilon_0 E_0 ^2 ⟨u⟩=21​ϵ0​E02​

    ⟨E02cos⁡2(kz−ωt+δ)⟩=E022=E02≈0.7E0 \sqrt{\langle E_0 ^2 \cos ^2 (kz - \omega t + \delta) \rangle } = \sqrt{\frac{E_0^2}{2}} = \frac{E_0}{\sqrt{2}} \approx 0.7 E_0 ⟨E02​cos2(kz−ωt+δ)⟩​=2E02​​​=2​E0​​≈0.7E0​

    The "intensity" of the electromagnetic wave is defined as its power per unit area, or energy per unit area per unit time

    I=powerarea=energyarea⋅time I = \frac{\text{power}}{\text{area}} = \frac{\text{energy}}{\text{area}\cdot\text{time}} I=areapower​=area⋅timeenergy​

    If the light hits the surface of a perfect absorber, it will transfer its momentum to the surface. In a time Δt \Delta t Δt the momentum transfer will be

    Δp=⟨g⟩AcΔt \Delta \vec p = \langle \vec g \rangle A c \Delta t Δp=⟨g⟩AcΔt

    so the radiation pressure (average force per unit area) is

    P=1AΔpΔt=12ϵ0E02=Ic P = \frac{1}{A} \frac{\Delta p}{\Delta t} = \frac{1}{2} \epsilon_0 E_0 ^2 = \frac{I}{c} P=A1​ΔtΔp​=21​ϵ0​E02​=cI​

    Of course, when falling on a perfect reflector, the radiation pressure is twice as big, since the resulting momentum of the reflected light switches direction instead of being absorbed.

    Example Problem 9.10

    The intensity of sunlight hitting the earth is about 1300 W/m2 W/m^2 W/m2. If sunlight strikes a perfect absorber, what pressure does it exert? How about a perfect reflector? What fraction of atmospheric pressure does this amount to?

    The radiation pressure for a perfect absorber is

    P=Ic=13003⋅108≈4.3⋅10−6N/m2 P = \frac{I}{c} = \frac{1300}{3 \cdot 10^8} \approx 4.3 \cdot 10^{-6} N/m^2 P=cI​=3⋅1081300​≈4.3⋅10−6N/m2

    The atmospheric pressure on earth's surface is about 105N 10^5 N 105N, so

    Radiation pressureAtmos pressure≈10−11 \frac{\text{Radiation pressure}}{\text{Atmos pressure}} \approx 10^{-11} Atmos pressureRadiation pressure​≈10−11

    The resulting radiation pressure is tiny compared with the normal pressures we're used to, but in space where atmospheric pressure is absent the result can be significant, and laser beams on individual atoms with tiny masses can slow and trap individual particles via radiation pressure.
