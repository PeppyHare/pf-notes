    8.2: Momentum

    In this chapter we talk about:

    • Electromagnetic momentum
    • Maxwell stress tensor
    • Conservation of electromagnetic momentum

    8.2.1: Electromagnetic Momentum

    As it turns out, if you disregard the momentum associated with electromagnetic fields, Newton's laws appear not to work out! Consider a basic system in cartesian coordinates of two moving point charges:

    What happens between the two charges? Well, the magnetic field of q1 q_1 q1​ points into the page at q2 q_2 q2​, so the magnetic force on q2 q_2 q2​ is to the right, and the magnetic field of q2 q_2 q2​ is out of the page at q1 q_1 q1​ , so the magnetic force on q1 q_1 q1​ is upward. The net electric force between the two charges is repulsive and opposite, but the magnetic forces aren't, so the electromagnetic force on q1 q_1 q1​ on q2 q_2 q2​ is equal but not opposite to the force of q2 q_2 q2​ on q1 q_1 q1​, in violation of Newton's third law! We've got a problem, and we're going to solve it by invoking the momentum of the EM field.

    8.2.2 The Maxwell Stress Tensor

    The way to recover conservation of momentum proceeds the same way we recovered the conservation of energy via the Poynting vector. Starting with the basic Coulomb/Lorentz laws, we'll write down an expression for the electromagnetic force on charges in a volume. We're going to integrate that over all space, which can have any distribution of charge, and relate that expression for force to an expression which only involves the field. In the interest of brevity, we'll skip around a bit and leave the full derivations for the real textbook.

    Suppose we have a volume V V V containing some distribution of charge, current, and electromagnetic fields. The total force on that volume is

    F=∫V(E+v×B)ρdτ(dq=ρdτ)=∫V(ρE+J×B)dτ(J=ρv) \vec{F} = \int_V ( \vec E + \vec v \cross \vec B) \rho \dd \tau \qquad (\dd q = \rho \dd \tau) \\ = \int _V (\rho \vec E + \vec J \cross \vec B ) \dd \tau \qquad (\vec J = \rho \vec v) F=∫V​(E+v×B)ρdτ(dq=ρdτ)=∫V​(ρE+J×B)dτ(J=ρv)

    Again, the goal is to replace anything that looks like a source in favor of fields, using Maxwell's equations. It's handy to define the force per unit volume f f f:

    f≡ρE+J×B \vec f \equiv \rho \vec E + \vec J \cross \vec B f≡ρE+J×B

    ρ=ϵ0(∇⋅E)(Gauss’ Law)J=1μ0∇×B−ϵ0∂E∂t \rho = \epsilon_0 ( \div \vec E ) \quad \text{(Gauss' Law)} \\ \vec J = \frac{1}{\mu_0} \curl \vec B - \epsilon_0 \pdv{\vec E}{t} ρ=ϵ0​(∇⋅E)(Gauss’ Law)J=μ0​1​∇×B−ϵ0​∂t∂E​

    Skipping through a few steps, we cut to the chase. Similar to the derivation of the Poynting theorem, also using the other two Maxwell equations we haven't yet, we get

    f=∇⋅T‾−ϵ0μ0∂S∂t \vec f = \div \overline{\vec T} - \epsilon_0 \mu_0 \pdv{\vec S}{t} f=∇⋅T−ϵ0​μ0​∂t∂S​

    T‾≡(TxxTxyTxzTyxTyyTyzTzxTzyTzz)Tij≡ϵ0(EiEj−12δijE2)+1μ0(BiBj−12δijB2) \overline{\vec T} \equiv \begin{pmatrix} T_{xx} & T_{xy} & T_{xz} \\ T_{yx} & T_{yy} & T_{yz} \\ T_{zx} & T_{zy} & T_{zz} \end{pmatrix} \\ T_{ij} \equiv \epsilon_0 \left( E_i E_j - \frac{1}{2} \delta_{ij} E^2 \right) + \frac{1}{\mu_0} \left(B_i B_j - \frac{1}{2} \delta_{ij} B^2 \right) T≡⎝⎜⎛​Txx​Tyx​Tzx​​Txy​Tyy​Tzy​​Txz​Tyz​Tzz​​⎠⎟⎞​Tij​≡ϵ0​(Ei​Ej​−21​δij​E2)+μ0​1​(Bi​Bj​−21​δij​B2)

    where S \vec S S is the Poynting vector and T‾ \overline{\vec T} T is the so-called "Maxwell stress tensor." To keep in mind what kind of units we're talking about here, f \vec f f has units force per unit volume, and the divergence will strip one spatial dimension, so the Maxwell stress tensor will have units of stress (force per unit area).

    The tensor has diagonal "pressure" terms and off-diagonal "shear" terms. For "pressure" forces, the force and area are in the same direction, and in the "shear" case the force and area are orthogonal.

    The divergence term ∇⋅T‾ \div \overline{\vec T} ∇⋅T is itself a vector

    F=d pmechd t=−ϵ0μ0d d t∫VSdτ+∮T‾⋅da \vec F = \dv{\vec{p}_{mech}}{t} = - \epsilon_0 \mu_0 \dv{}{t} \int_V \vec S \dd \tau + \oint \overline{\vec T} \cdot \dd \vec a F=dtdpmech​​=−ϵ0​μ0​dtd​∫V​Sdτ+∮T⋅da

    The first term on the right is related to the momentum stored in the electromagnetic field. The second term is the rate at which momentum flows across the surface, and we describe the left-hand-side as the rate of change of the momentum of charges within the volume.

    We identify another useful term as the first integrand on the right:

    g≡ϵ0μ0S=ϵ0(E×B)(momentum density in EM fields) \vec g \equiv \epsilon_0 \mu_0 \vec S = \epsilon_0 (\vec E \cross B) \quad \text{(momentum density in EM fields)} g≡ϵ0​μ0​S=ϵ0​(E×B)(momentum density in EM fields)

    which is the momentum density within the fields. Just as a note, the signs here are swapped from the Poynting theorem - the Maxwell stress tensor is defined such that momentum flowing into the region corresponds with increasing T‾ \overline{\vec T} T, and vice-versa, opposite the case we had with S \vec S S.

    In a charge-free region,

    −d d t∫Vgdτ+∮ST‾⋅da=0 - \dv{}{t} \int_V \vec g \dd \tau + \oint_S \overline{\vec T} \cdot \dd \vec a = 0 −dtd​∫V​gdτ+∮S​T⋅da=0

    and since the above is true for all regions V V V, we have our familiar continuity-type equation

    −∂g∂t+∇⋅T‾=0 - \pdv{\vec g}{t} + \div \overline{\vec T} = 0 −∂t∂g​+∇⋅T=0

    8.2.4 Angular Momentum in EM Fields

    As a reminder, we associate with the electromagnetic fields an energy density

    uem=12ϵ0E2+μ02B2 u_{em} = \frac{1}{2} \epsilon_0 E^2 + \frac{\mu_0}{2} B^2 uem​=21​ϵ0​E2+2μ0​​B2

    and a momentum density

    g=ϵ0(E×B) \vec g = \epsilon_0 ( \vec E \cross \vec B) g=ϵ0​(E×B)

    For that matter, we define angular momentum in the normal fashion

    l=r×g=ϵ0[r×(E×B)] \vec l = \vec r \cross \vec g = \epsilon_0 [ \vec r \cross ( \vec E \cross \vec B) ] l=r×g=ϵ0​[r×(E×B)]

    where the presence of r \vec r r means it's defined about some point or axis.

    Example 8.4

    Imagine a very long solenoid with radius R R R, n n n turns per unit length, and current I I I. Coaxial with the solenoid are two long cylindrical (non-conducting) shells of length l l l - one inside the solenoid at radius a a a carries a charge +Q +Q +Q distributed uniformly over its surface; the other outside the solenoid at radius b b b carries charge −Q -Q −Q . When the current in the solenoid is gradually reduced to nothing, the cylinders begin to rotate. Question: where does the angular momentum come from?

    We'll start by writing down the initial angular momentum of the system, then see what happens when we start to change the current. It is assumed that the solenoid is very long compared with the charged cylinders, and that the length of each charged cylinder is much much greater than its radius.

    The initial E \vec E E is given by looking at a Gaussian cylinder between the two charged cylinders

    B={μ0nIz^0<s<R0,s>R \vec B = \begin{cases} \mu_0 n I \vu z & \qquad 0 < s < R \\ 0, & \qquad s > R \end{cases} B={μ0​nIz^0,​0<s<Rs>R​
