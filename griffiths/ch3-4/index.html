    3.4: Multipole Expansion

    3.4.1: Approximate Potentials at Large Distances

    If you are very far away from a localized charge distribution, it "looks" like a point charge, and the potential is - to good approximation - (1/4πϵ0)Q/r (1/4 \pi \epsilon_0) Q/r (1/4πϵ0​)Q/r, where Q Q Q is the total charge. We have often used this as a check on formulas for V. But what if Q Q Q is zero? You might reply that the potential is then approximately zero, and of course, you're right in a sense (indeed, the potential at large r is pretty small even if Q is not zero). But we're looking for something a bit more informative than that.

    Example 3.10

    This is the desired result - the multipole expansion of V V V in powers of 1/r 1/r 1/r. If you are interested in the potential along the z′ z' z′ axis (or - putting it the other way round - if you orient your r′ \vec{r'} r′ coordinates so the z′ z' z′ axis lies along r \vec{r} r), then α \alpha α is the usual polar angle θ′ \theta' θ′. As it stands, (3.95) \eqref{3.95} (3.95) is exact, but it is useful primarily as an approximation scheme: the lowest nonzero term in the expansion provides the approximate potential at large r, and the successive terms tell us how to improve the approximation if greater precision is required.

    3.4.2: The Monopole and Dipole Terms

    Ordinarily, the multipole expansion is dominated (at large r) by the monopole term:

    Vmon(r)=14πϵ0Qr V_{mon} (\vec{r}) = \frac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_0} \frac{Q}{r} Vmon​(r)=4πϵ0​1​rQ​

    where Q=∫ρdτ Q =\int \rho \dd{\tau} Q=∫ρdτ is the total charge of the configuration. This is just what we expect for the approximate potential at large distances from the charge. For a point charge at the origin, Vmon V_{mon} Vmon​ is the exact potential, not merely a first approximation at large r; in this case, all the higher multipoles vanish. If the total charge is zero, the dominant term in the potential will be the dipole (unless, of course, it also vanishes):

    Vdip(r)=14πϵ01r2∫r′cos⁡αρ(r′)dτ′ V_{dip}(\vec{r}) = \frac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_0} \frac{1}{r^2} \int r' \cos \alpha \rho(\vec{r'}) \dd{\tau'} Vdip​(r)=4πϵ0​1​r21​∫r′cosαρ(r′)dτ′

    Since α \alpha α is the angle between r′ r' r′ and r r r (Fig 2.38),

    r′cos⁡α=r^⋅r′ r' \cos \alpha = \vu{r} \cdot \vec{r'} r′cosα=r^⋅r′

    and the dipole potential can be written more succinctly:

    Vdip(r)=14πϵ01r2r^⋅∫r′ρ(r′)dτ′ V_{dip}(\vec{r}) = \frac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_0} \frac{1}{r^2} \vu{r} \cdot \int \vec{r'} \rho(\vec{r'}) \dd{\tau'} Vdip​(r)=4πϵ0​1​r21​r^⋅∫r′ρ(r′)dτ′

    This integral (which does not depend on r \vec{r} r ) is called the dipole moment of the distribution:

    p≡∫r′ρ(r′)dτ′(3.98) \vec{p} \equiv \int \vec{r'} \rho(\vec{r'}) \dd{\tau'} \tagl{3.98} p≡∫r′ρ(r′)dτ′(3.98)

    and the dipole contribution to the potential simplifies to

    Vdip(r)=14πϵ0p⋅r^r2(3.99) V_{dip}(\vec{r}) = \frac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_0} \frac{\vec{p} \cdot \vu{r}}{r^2} \tagl{3.99} Vdip​(r)=4πϵ0​1​r2p⋅r^​(3.99)

    The dipole moment is determined by the geometry (size, shape, and density) of the charge distribution. (3.98) \eqref{3.98} (3.98) translates in the usual way (Sect 2.1.4) for point, line, and surface charges. Thus, the dipole moment for a collection of point charges is

    p=∑i=1nqir′i(3.100) \vec{p} = \sum_{i=1} ^n q_i \vec{r'}_i \tagl{3.100} p=i=1∑n​qi​r′i​(3.100)

    For a physical dipole (equal and opposite charges ±q \pm q ±q),

    p=qr+′−qr−′=q(r+′−r−′)=qd(3.101) \vec{p} = q\vec{r'_+} - q \vec{r_- ' } = q(\vec{r' _+} - \vec{r'_-}) = q \vec{d} \tagl{3.101} p=qr+′​−qr−′​=q(r+′​−r−′​)=qd(3.101)

    where d \vec{d} d is the vector from the negative charge to the positive one (Fig. 3.29).

    Is this consistent with what we got in Example 3.10? Yes: If you put (3.101) \eqref{3.101} (3.101) into (3.99) \eqref{3.99} (3.99), you recover (3.90) \eqref{3.90} (3.90). Notice, however, that this is only the approximate potential of the physical dipole - evidently there are higher multipole contributions. Of course, as you go farther and farther away, Vdip V_{dip} Vdip​ becomes a better and better approximation, since the higher terms die off more rapidly with increasing r r r. By the same token, at a fixed r r r the dipole approximation improves as you shrink the separation distance d d d. To construct a perfect dipole whose potential is given exactly by (3.99) \eqref{3.99} (3.99), you'd have to let d d d approach zero. Unfortunately you then lose the dipole term too, unless you simultaneously arrange for q to go to infinity! A physical dipole becomes a pure dipole, then, in the rather artificial limit d→0.q→∞ d \rightarrow 0. q \rightarrow \infty d→0.q→∞, with the product qd=p qd = p qd=p held fixed. When someone uses the word "dipole," you can't always tell whether they mean a physical dipole (with finite separation between the charges) or an ideal dipole. If in doubt, assume that d is small enough that you can safely apply (3.99) \eqref{3.99} (3.99).

    Dipole moments are vectors, and they add accordingly: if you have two dipoles p1 \vec{p_1} p1​ and p2 \vec{p_2} p2​, the total dipole moment is p1+p2 \vec{p_1} + \vec{p_2} p1​+p2​. For instance, with four charges at the corners of a square, as shown in Fig. 3.30, the net dipole moment is zero. You can see this by combining the charges in pairs or by adding up the four contributions individually using (3.100) \eqref{3.100} (3.100). This is a quadrupole, as I indicated earlier, and its potential is dominated by the quadrupole term in the multipole expansion.

    3.4.3: Origin of Coordinates in Multipole Expansions

    I mentioned earlier that a point charge at the origin constitutes a "pure" monopole. If it is not at the origin, it's no longer a pure monopole. For instance, the charge in Fig. 3.32 has a dipole moment p=qdy^ \vec{p} = q d \vu{y} p=qdy^​, and a corresponding dipole term in its potential. The monopole potential (1/4πϵ0)q/r (1/4 \pi \epsilon_0) q/r (1/4πϵ0​)q/r is not quite correct for this configuration; rather, the exact potential is (1/4πϵ0)q/γ (1/4 \pi \epsilon_0) q/\gr (1/4πϵ0​)q/γ. The multipole expansion is, remember, a series in inverse powers of r (the distance to the origin), and when we expand 1/γ 1/\gr 1/γ, we get all powers, not just the first.

    So moving the origin (or, what amounts to the same thing, moving the charge) can radically alter a multipole expansion. The monopole moment Q Q Q does not change, since the total charge is obviously independent of the coordinate system. (In Fig. 3.32, the monopole term was unaffected when we moved q away from the origin - it's just that it was no longer the whole story: a dipole term - and for that matter all higher poles - appeared as well.) Ordinarily, the dipole moment does change when you shift the origin, but there is an important exception: If the total charge is zero, then the dipole moment is independent of the choice of origin. For suppose we displace the origin by an amount a \vec{a} a (Fig. 3.33). The new dipole moment is then

    p2=∫r′ρ(r′)dτ′=∫(r′−a)ρ(r′)dτ′=∫r′ρ(r′)dτ′−a∫ρ(r′)dτ′=p−Qa \begin{aligned} \vec{p_2} & = \int \vec{r'} \rho(\vec{r'}) \dd{\tau'} = \int (\vec{r'} - \vec{a} ) \rho (\vec{r'}) \dd{\tau'} \\ & = \int \vec{r'} \rho(\vec{r'}) \dd{\tau'} - \vec{a} \int \rho(\vec{r'}) \dd{\tau'} = \vec{p} - Q \vec{a} \end{aligned} p2​​=∫r′ρ(r′)dτ′=∫(r′−a)ρ(r′)dτ′=∫r′ρ(r′)dτ′−a∫ρ(r′)dτ′=p−Qa​

    In particular, if Q=0 Q = 0 Q=0, the p2=p \vec{p_2} = \vec{p} p2​=p. So if someone asks for the dipole moment in Fig 3.34(a), you can answer with confidence "qd q \vec{d} qd," but if you're asked for the dipole moment in Fig 3.34(b), the appropriate response would be "With respect to what origin?"

    3.4.4: The Electric Field of a Dipole

    So far we have only worked with potentials. Now I would like to calculate the electric field of a (perfect) dipole. If we choose coordinates so that p \vec{p} p is at the origin and points in the z direction (Fig. 3.36), then the potential at r,θ r, \theta r,θ is (3.99) \eqref{3.99} (3.99):

    Vdip(r,θ)=r^⋅p4πϵ0r2=pcos⁡θ4πϵ0r2(3.102) V_{dip} (r, \theta) = \frac{\vu{r} \cdot \vec{p}}{4 \pi \epsilon_0 r^2} = \frac{p \cos \theta}{4 \pi \epsilon_0 r^2} \tagl{3.102} Vdip​(r,θ)=4πϵ0​r2r^⋅p​=4πϵ0​r2pcosθ​(3.102)

    To get the field, we take the negative gradient of V V V:

    Er=−∂V∂r=2pcos⁡θ4πϵ0r3Eθ=−1r∂V∂θ=psin⁡θ4πϵ0r3Eϕ=−1rsin⁡θ∂V∂ϕ=0 \begin{aligned} E_r & = - \pdv{V}{r} = \frac{2 p \cos \theta}{4 \pi \epsilon_0 r^3} \\ E_\theta & = - \frac{1}{r} \pdv{V}{\theta} = \frac{p \sin \theta}{4 \pi \epsilon_0 r^3} \\ E_\phi & = - \frac{1}{r \sin \theta} \pdv{V}{\phi} = 0 \end{aligned} Er​Eθ​Eϕ​​=−∂r∂V​=4πϵ0​r32pcosθ​=−r1​∂θ∂V​=4πϵ0​r3psinθ​=−rsinθ1​∂ϕ∂V​=0​

    Thus,

    Edip(r,θ)=p4πϵ0r3(2cos⁡θr^+sin⁡θθ^)(3.103) \vec{E_{dip}} (r, \theta) = \frac{p}{4 \pi \epsilon_0 r^3}(2 \cos \theta \vu{r} + \sin \theta \vu{\theta}) \tagl{3.103} Edip​(r,θ)=4πϵ0​r3p​(2cosθr^+sinθθ^)(3.103)

    This formula makes explicit reference to a particular coordinate system (spherical) and assumes a particular orientation for p \vec{p} p (along z). It can be recast in a coordinate-free form, analogous to the potential in (3.99) \eqref{3.99} (3.99) - See problem 3.36.

    Notice that the dipole falls off as the inverse cube of r; the monopole field (Q/4πϵ0r2)r^ (Q / 4 \pi \epsilon_0 r^2) \vu{r} (Q/4πϵ0​r2)r^ goes as the inverse square, of course. Quadrupole fields go like 1/r4 1/r^4 1/r4, octopole like 1/r5 1/r^5 1/r5, and so on. (This merely reflects how the respective potentials fall off - the gradient introduces another factor of 1/r 1/r 1/r ).

    Figure 3.37(a) shows the field lines of a "pure" dipole (3.103) \eqref{3.103} (3.103). For comparison, I have also sketched the field lines for a "physical" dipole, in Fig 3.37(b). Notice how similar the two pictures become if you blot out the central region; up close, however, they are entirely different. Only for points r≫d r \gg d r≫d does (3.103) \eqref{3.103} (3.103) represent a valid approximation to the field of a physical dipole. As I mentioned earlier, this regime can be reached either by going to large r r r or by squeezing the charges very close together.
