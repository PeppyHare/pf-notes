    10.3 Point Charges

    10.3.1 Moving Point Charges: Lienard-Wiechert Potentials

    Suppose we have a point charge moving on some trajectory w(t) \vec w(t) w(t). If we naively read off the retarded potential

    V(r,t)=14πϵ0∫ρ(r′,tr)γdτ′ V(\vec r, t) = \frac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_0} \int \frac{\rho(\vec r', t_r)}{\gr} \dd \tau' V(r,t)=4πϵ0​1​∫γρ(r′,tr​)​dτ′

    14πϵ0qγ \frac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_0} \frac{q}{\gr} 4πϵ0​1​γq​

    But that's wrong for a subtle reason: It is true that for a point source γ \gr γ comes outside the integral, but the remainder

    ∫ρ(r′,tr)dτ′ \int \rho(\vec r', t_r) \dd \tau' ∫ρ(r′,tr​)dτ′

    is not equal to the charge of the particle, and depends on the location of the point r \vec r r. To calculate the total charge of a configuration, you must integrate ρ \rho ρ over the entire distribution at one instant of time, but here the retardation tr=t−γ/c t_r = t - \gr / c tr​=t−γ/c, obliges us to evaluate ρ \rho ρ at different times for different parts of the configuration. If the source is moving, this will give a distorted picture of the total charge. You might think that this problem would disappear for point charges, but it doesn't. In Maxwell's electrodynamics, formulated as it is in terms of charge and current densities, a point charge must be regarded as the limit of an extended charge, when the size goes to zero. And for an extended particle, no matter how small, the retardation throws a factor (1−γ^⋅v/c)−1 (1 - \vu \gr \cdot \vec v / c)^{-1} (1−γ^​⋅v/c)−1 where v \vec v v is the velocity of the charge at the retarded time

    ∫ρ(r′,tr)dτ′=q1−γ^⋅v/c \int \rho ( \vec r ', t_r) \dd \tau' = \frac{q}{1 - \vu \gr \cdot \vec v / c} ∫ρ(r′,tr​)dτ′=1−γ^​⋅v/cq​

    Proof: ∫ρ(r′,tr)dτ′=q1−γ^⋅v/c \int \rho ( \vec r ', t_r) \dd \tau' = \frac{q}{1 - \vu \gr \cdot \vec v / c} ∫ρ(r′,tr​)dτ′=1−γ^​⋅v/cq​

    This is a purely geometrical effect, and it may help to tell the story in a less abstract context. You will not have noticed it, for obvious reasons, the the fact is that a train coming towards you looks a little longer than it really is, because the light you receive from the caboose left earlier than the light you receive simultaneously from the engine, and at that earlier time the train was farther away (Fig 10.6).     L′c=L′−LvorL′=L1−v/c \frac{L'}{c} = \frac{L' - L}{v} \quad \text{or} \quad L' = \frac{L}{1-v/c} cL′​=vL′−L​orL′=1−v/cL​

    So approaching trains appear longer, by a factor (1−v/c)−1 (1 - v/c)^{-1} (1−v/c)−1. By contrast, a train going away from you looks shorter, by a factor (1+v/c)−1 (1 + v/c)^{-1} (1+v/c)−1.
